# Draft Reference

Describe the content that an effective draft should contain.

Before writing a draft, you MUST know:

- the problem or opportunity being explored
- the audience that needs alignment
- the constraints or prior context that readers will need

## Iron Laws

- MUST keep the draft exploratory without pretending the answer is already final
- MUST make the problem, candidate directions, and open questions easy to review quickly
- MUST separate confirmed facts, assumptions, and unknowns clearly
- NEVER jump into detailed implementation too early
- NEVER hide the open questions that reviewers need to resolve

## Draft Structure

Cover the following sections, including optional sections when relevant.

- Overview: one-paragraph summary of the topic and why it matters now
- Audience `[Optional]`: who needs this draft and what decision or alignment they need from it
- Background: context, prior work, and relevant constraints
- Problem Statement: the core problem or opportunity being discussed
- Goals: the outcomes this draft is trying to achieve
- Non-Goals: what this draft intentionally does not solve yet
- Candidate Directions: the promising ideas, options, or approaches under consideration
- Open Questions: unresolved items that block stronger commitment
- Risks and Assumptions: known uncertainties, dependencies, and working assumptions
- Stakeholders `[Optional]`: people or teams affected by the draft
- Dependencies `[Optional]`: external work, approvals, or assumptions that shape the draft
- Timeline `[Optional]`: milestone notes if timing matters to the discussion
- Related References `[Optional]`: links to earlier drafts, notes, or related documents
- Next Steps: what should happen after the draft is reviewed

## Next Steps

After the draft is reviewed, the usual follow-up is a spec, a design, or an ADR depending on whether the next need is requirements, implementation planning, or decision capture.
