# Best Practices Reference

Describe the content that an effective best-practices document should contain.

Before writing best practices, you MUST know:

- the problem area or workflow the guidance applies to
- the recurring mistakes or review friction points
- the trade-offs that make some advice situational rather than absolute

## Iron Laws

- MUST provide clear defaults that help readers make repeated decisions consistently
- MUST explain why recommended patterns are useful and why anti-patterns are risky
- MUST keep the guidance reusable instead of tied to only one case
- NEVER present preferences as rigid rules without context
- NEVER list too many rules for readers to apply in practice
- MUST use web search to find up-to-date and relevant docs, examples, and tutorials to inform best-practices content

## Best Practices Structure

Cover the following sections, including optional sections when relevant.

- Overview: the problem area the practices apply to
- Audience `[Optional]`: who should use this guidance and where it applies
- Scope: where the guidance applies and where it does not
- Core Principles: the few durable ideas behind the recommendations
- Recommended Practices: the concrete things readers should usually do
- Anti-Patterns: common mistakes and why they are risky
- Decision Heuristics: simple rules for choosing among options
- Examples: short examples that show the guidance in context
- Exceptions and Trade-Offs: when it is reasonable to deviate
- Review Checklist `[Optional]`: a compact checklist for applying the guidance during review or delivery
- Migration Advice `[Optional]`: how to move existing work toward the recommended patterns
- Team-Specific Notes `[Optional]`: local conventions that matter in this context
- Related References `[Optional]`: links to guides, specs, or designs that go deeper
